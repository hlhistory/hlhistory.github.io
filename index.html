Writing Tips

Paper 3 — 3 essays, 2.5 hrs
IB command terms
To What Extent
Give a direct answer, weigh evidence on both sides, qualify your conclusion
Examine
Describe AND explain causes/consequences — don't just list events
Discuss
Present multiple perspectives, weigh their merits, reach a judgement
Compare & Contrast
Similarities AND differences — interweave, don't split into two halves
Evaluate
Judge significance, success, or validity — requires evidence for your judgement
Assess
Weigh importance of factors — show awareness of competing views
Essay structure (PEEL per paragraph)
Intro

Direct Thesis First

  • Answer the question in sentence 1 — don't build up to it
  • Name your 2–3 key arguments
  • Drop one historiographical school to signal critical thinking
  • Avoid "Since the dawn of time..." or "This essay will explore..."
Body Paragraphs — PEEL

Point → Evidence → Explain → Link

  • Point: One clear argument that answers the question
  • Evidence: Specific date, name, event, statistic
  • Explain: Why does this evidence support your point?
  • Link: Connect back to the thesis
Historiography

Name → Define → Apply → Qualify

  • Name the school, not just "historians say"
  • Explain what the school argues in one sentence
  • Apply it to the specific question with evidence
  • Push back — "However, this overlooks..."
Conclusion

Qualify, Don't Just Repeat

  • Restate thesis with nuance — "While X was crucial, Y ultimately..."
  • Note the historical debate — "Revisionist historians challenge..."
  • Avoid "In conclusion, I have shown..." — just conclude
  • End on a historical judgement, not a summary
Dos and don'ts
Do
  • Open with a direct answer to the question
  • Use specific dates — not "the 1960s"
  • Acknowledge counterarguments before dismissing them
  • Deploy at least 2 historiographical perspectives
  • Quote or paraphrase a named historian
  • Show causation — "This resulted in... because..."
Don't
  • Narrate events — analyse them
  • Use vague language — "many people," "various reasons"
  • Write a one-sided essay with no counterargument
  • Spend time on pre-question context that isn't asked
  • Use bullet points in the essay itself
  • Confuse description for analysis
IB mark criteria (Paper 3)
Criterion A — 6 marks

Knowledge & Understanding

Specific dates, names, events, places. Accurate and relevant. Avoid errors — one factual mistake costs marks.

Criterion B — 6 marks

Critical Thinking

Analysis, synthesis, evaluation. Historiographical debate. Multiple perspectives. Comparing causes, effects, significance.

Criterion C — 6 marks

Structure

Clear thesis, logical progression, coherent argument throughout, strong conclusion that returns to the thesis.

Criterion D — 2 marks

Style of Communication

Clear, academic prose. Correct terminology. Appropriate essay format — no lists, no bullet points.

Useful analytical phrases
  • "To a significant extent..." — opens a nuanced "to what extent" answer
  • "However, the [x] interpretation overlooks..." — signals critical thinking
  • "This was a necessary but not sufficient cause because..." — sophisticated causation
  • "While orthodox historians argue X, the revisionist view holds Y..." — deploys historiography
  • "The evidence suggests a more complex picture..." — avoids oversimplification
  • "This must be contextualised within..." — shows awareness of broader forces
  • "A subaltern reading of this event reveals..." — deploys perspective directly

Civil Rights — The Americas

1950s – 1970s
Context: Black Americans lived under Jim Crow — legally enforced segregation in the South since Reconstruction. The 1896 Plessy v. Ferguson ruling declared "separate but equal" constitutional. After WWII, Black veterans returned from fighting fascism abroad to face segregation at home — creating powerful moral contradictions that drove the movement. The Cold War also mattered: US government was embarrassed by visible racism undermining its claim to represent freedom globally.

Legal Foundations & Early Challenges (1909–1955)

▾
1909NAACP founded — Legal strategy: fight segregation through the courts. Thurgood Marshall becomes lead litigator.
1941A. Philip Randolph threatens March on Washington — forces FDR to issue Executive Order 8802 banning racial discrimination in defence industries. Shows political leverage.
1948Truman desegregates the US military (Executive Order 9981) — under pressure from Randolph; first major federal step.
1954Brown v. Board of Education — Supreme Court unanimously overturns Plessy. Thurgood Marshall argues the case. "Separate is inherently unequal." The NAACP's biggest legal victory — but implementation required mass protest.
1955Emmett Till murdered — 14-year-old tortured and killed in Mississippi. Killers acquitted by all-white jury. Open casket funeral galvanises the nation.
1955–56Montgomery Bus Boycott — Rosa Parks arrested. MLK emerges as national leader. 381-day boycott. Supreme Court rules bus segregation unconstitutional. Key: economic pressure + nonviolent discipline + legal challenge working together.

Direct Action — The Movement's Peak (1960–1963)

▾
1960Greensboro Sit-ins — 4 Black students at Woolworth's; spreads to 55 cities in weeks. SNCC founded. Shows the power of disciplined youth-led protest.
1961Freedom Rides — CORE and SNCC test desegregation on interstate buses. Mob violence in Anniston, Birmingham. Attorney General Robert Kennedy forced to act. The strategy: provoke violence → force federal response → expose Southern racism to the world.
1963Birmingham Campaign — MLK deliberately provokes Police Commissioner "Bull" Connor. Fire hoses and police dogs on peaceful protesters. Global TV coverage forces JFK to propose civil rights legislation.
Aug 1963March on Washington — 250,000 marchers. MLK: "I Have a Dream." Largest US demonstration to date. John Lewis (SNCC) gives fiery speech. Demonstrates the movement's breadth — but also tensions between moderate and radical wings.
Sep 196316th Street Baptist Church bombing — KKK kills four girls: Addie Mae Collins, Cynthia Wesley, Carole Robertson, Carol Denise McNair. Shocks even moderate white opinion.

Legislation & Selma (1964–1965)

▾
1964Civil Rights Act — Banned discrimination in public spaces and employment. Signed by LBJ. Three-week Senate filibuster (60+ days) — longest in US history. Significant, but didn't address voting rights or structural poverty.
1964Freedom Summer — 1,000 student volunteers register Black voters in Mississippi. James Chaney, Andrew Goodman, Michael Schwerner murdered by KKK-police. FBI knew beforehand.
Mar 1965"Bloody Sunday" — Selma — State troopers beat marchers on Edmund Pettus Bridge. John Lewis suffered fractured skull. Televised nationally; LBJ addresses Congress: "We Shall Overcome." Arguably the most effective single image of the movement — forced the Voting Rights Act through.
1965Voting Rights Act — Outlawed discriminatory voting practices (literacy tests, poll taxes). Within 2 years: Black voter registration in Mississippi rose from 6.7% to 59.8%.

Black Power, Radicalism & Limits (1965–1975)

▾
Key tension: The Civil Rights Act and Voting Rights Act addressed legal segregation — but did nothing for structural economic inequality, police brutality, and Northern de facto segregation. This gap produced the Black Power movement.
1965Watts Riots (Los Angeles) — 6 days of unrest; 34 dead; $40M damage. Exposed the limits of legislative victories — the urban North was untouched by the Civil Rights Acts.
1966Stokely Carmichael coins "Black Power" — shifts from integration to Black pride, community control, and self-defence. SNCC expels white members. This is the moment "the movement" fractured ideologically.
1966Black Panther Party founded — Huey Newton and Bobby Seale in Oakland. Armed self-defence, free breakfast programs, community clinics. FBI's COINTELPRO targeted it aggressively.
Apr 1968MLK assassinated in Memphis while supporting sanitation workers' strike — shows his shift toward economic justice beyond civil rights. 100+ cities erupt in riots.
1968–72American Indian Movement (AIM) founded — Native American civil rights; Alcatraz occupation (1969); Bureau of Indian Affairs occupation (1972); Wounded Knee standoff (1973).
1968–70Chicano Movement — César Chávez and UFW; student walkouts ("Blowouts"); National Chicano Moratorium 1970. Connects civil rights to labour rights in California and Southwest.

Essay angles
Why did the movement succeed in passing legislation?

Three intersecting factors: media strategy (Birmingham's televised violence forced federal action), political timing (LBJ needed civil rights as part of Great Society and to contrast with segregationist Democrats), and mass mobilisation (the movement's discipline made it impossible to dismiss as lawless). Argue: it was the combination, not any single factor.

Was the Civil Rights Movement a success?

Two-part argument: Yes — legally dismantled Jim Crow; voting rights restored; Black representation increased. But — structural economic inequality unchanged; Black poverty rates barely shifted; Kerner Commission (1968) found two Americas. Black Power advocates saw legal equality as insufficient without economic justice.

Role of women in the movement

Grassroots/subaltern angle: Ella Baker built the NAACP and SCLC from below, mentored SNCC, argued against charismatic leadership. Jo Ann Robinson organized the Montgomery boycott before Parks's arrest. Fannie Lou Hamer fought for Mississippi Freedom Democratic Party credentials. Argument: women did the organizing work that male leaders got credit for.

Liberation Theology

Latin America · 1960s–1990s
Context: In 1960s Latin America, the Catholic Church controlled vast wealth while the majority of its parishioners lived in extreme poverty. Military dictatorships, US-backed regimes, and land inequality defined the continent. Vatican II (1962–65) opened the door to a new social role for the Church — Latin American theologians walked through it and built a theology rooted in the experience of the poor. The result was liberation theology: a fusion of Christian faith, social analysis (influenced by Marxism), and grassroots community organization.

Origins — Vatican II to Medellín (1962–1968)

▾
1962–65Vatican II (Second Vatican Council) — Pope John XXIII modernizes the Church. Document Gaudium et Spes ("Joy and Hope") declares the Church must engage with the world, especially the poor. Opens the door for social theology.
1965Dependency Theory emerges — economists like André Gunder Frank argue Latin America is kept poor by its economic relationship with the North. Provides liberation theologians with their structural analysis of poverty.
1968CELAM Medellín Conference — Latin American Bishops' Conference applies Vatican II to Latin America. Key concepts coined: "preferential option for the poor" and "structural sin" (the idea that economic and political systems — not just individuals — can be sinful). This is the founding document of liberation theology as a movement.
1968Paulo Freire — "Pedagogy of the Oppressed" — not a theologian, but hugely influential. "Conscientização" (consciousness-raising): educating the poor to understand the structures of their oppression. Informs the CEBs method.
1971Gustavo Gutiérrez — "Teología de la Liberación" — the foundational text. Peruvian priest and theologian. Argues: liberation has three inseparable dimensions — liberation from sin, liberation from socioeconomic oppression, and liberation as communion with God. Christian salvation is inseparable from social liberation.

Key Concepts to Know

▾
  • "Preferential option for the poor" — God takes the side of the marginalized; the Church must too. Not exclusive of others, but the poor are the primary concern.
  • "Structural sin" — Poverty is not just the result of individual failings; economic and political systems themselves can be unjust and sinful.
  • CEBs (Base Ecclesiastical Communities) — Small grassroots groups of 10–30 people who read the Bible in light of their daily oppression and organize for change. The institutional engine of liberation theology.
  • "Orthopraxis over orthodoxy" — Right action is as important as right belief. Faith must be lived, not just professed. Challenged the Church's traditional emphasis on doctrine.
  • "See, Judge, Act" — The method: see social reality (observe poverty/injustice), judge it through scripture (theological analysis), then act to change it (community organizing/resistance).
  • Marxist class analysis — Liberation theologians borrowed Marxist tools to diagnose structural inequality, without necessarily endorsing communist revolution. This was what alarmed the Vatican.

Key Figures & Events (1966–1992)

▾
1966Camilo Torres (Colombia) — Catholic priest who concluded nonviolent means were insufficient; joined ELN guerrillas; killed in his first combat engagement. Radical fringe of liberation theology; debated as a symbol or a warning.
1970sCEBs multiply in Brazil — By 1980s, an estimated 80,000–100,000 Base Communities in Brazil alone. Cardinal Arns of São Paulo shelters dissidents. Trade unions, landless workers' movements grow out of CEBs.
1979Sandinista Revolution (Nicaragua) — Sandinistas overthrow Somoza dictatorship. Several liberation theologians (Ernesto Cardenal, Fernando Cardenal, Miguel D'Escoto) join the government as ministers. Became the flashpoint of Vatican-liberation theology conflict.
1979CELAM Puebla Conference — Pope John Paul II opens by warning against politicizing the Church and "re-reading the Gospel as ideology." But the final document reaffirms the preferential option for the poor — partially a victory for liberation theologians.
Mar 1980Oscar Romero assassinated — Archbishop of El Salvador killed by a death squad while saying Mass, one day after calling on soldiers to disobey orders to kill civilians. Had evolved from conservative to liberation theologian. Becomes the movement's most powerful martyr. "In the name of God, I beg you, I implore you — stop the repression."
Dec 1980Four US churchwomen murdered — Ita Ford, Maura Clarke, Dorothy Kazel, Jean Donovan raped and killed by Salvadoran National Guard. US government attempted to blame the victims. Internationalises El Salvador's conflict.
Mar 1983JPII publicly rebukes Ernesto Cardenal — At Managua airport, the Pope wags his finger at the kneeling poet-priest. Iconic photograph. Symbolises Rome's opposition to liberation theologians holding political office.
1984Cardinal Ratzinger's "Instruction on Liberation Theology" — Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith criticises the use of Marxist class analysis as incompatible with Christianity. Labels some liberation theology "a fundamental threat." Ratzinger becomes Pope Benedict XVI in 2005.
1985Leonardo Boff silenced — Brazilian Franciscan and leading liberation theologian ordered to stop teaching and publishing for one year. Leaves the priesthood in 1992. Symbolises Vatican suppression.
Nov 1989Jesuit Massacre (El Salvador) — Salvadoran military kills 6 Jesuit priests, their housekeeper and her daughter at UCA. Deepens international pressure for peace negotiations. Civil war ends 1992.

Essay angles
Was liberation theology primarily a religious or political movement?

Both — and that was the point. Gutiérrez argued the separation of spiritual and material liberation was theologically false. However, in practice it had clear political effects: CEBs became spaces for organising resistance; liberation theologians in Nicaragua governed. Argue that framing it as "political" was itself a political act by its opponents (Vatican, Reagan administration) to delegitimize it.

How significant was Vatican opposition?

Limited in practice, significant symbolically. The Ratzinger Instruction and Boff's silencing slowed the movement's institutional growth. But CEBs continued to multiply; the Medellín documents were never formally revoked; the "preferential option for the poor" remains Catholic doctrine today. Francis (elected 2013) rehabilitated liberation theology — he is Latin American, was shaped by this tradition.

Romero as a case study in essay intros

Open with: "When Archbishop Oscar Romero was assassinated in March 1980 — shot through the heart while saying Mass — his killer was backed by forces that included US-trained officers and a government the US continued to fund. This moment encapsulates the collision of liberation theology, US Cold War policy, and Latin American repression that defined the region in the 1970s–80s."

Cuba — Revolution & Cold War

1952 – 1991
Context: By 1952, the US owned 40% of Cuba's sugar, most utilities, and telecommunications. Batista's dictatorship combined brutal repression (10,000+ tortured/killed) with casino culture for American tourists. 1 in 3 Cubans lived in extreme poverty. Cuba was the perfect storm: extreme inequality, US neo-colonialism, a corrupt ruler, and a charismatic opposition leader with a guerrilla strategy — and a 90-mile proximity to the US that made it a Cold War flashpoint.

Road to Revolution (1952–1959)

▾
1952Batista's coup — Cancels elections when polls show he will lose. Castro (a lawyer) challenges it in court — fails. Concludes force is necessary.
Jul 26, 1953Moncada Barracks attack — Castro leads 135 rebels; fails; 60+ killed or captured. Castro's trial speech: "History Will Absolve Me" — becomes the revolution's manifesto. Sentenced to 15 years.
1955Amnesty + Mexico exile — Castro released; goes to Mexico; meets Che Guevara (Argentine doctor, committed Marxist). Founds M-26-7 (July 26 Movement).
Dec 1956Granma landing — 82 men land in eastern Cuba; Batista's forces immediately attack; only 12 survive (including Fidel, Raúl, Che). Retreat to Sierra Maestra mountains.
1957–58Sierra Maestra campaign — Rebels earn peasant support; build a guerrilla army. Urban underground (M-26-7) sabotages Batista. Frank País organizes cities until his assassination (1957). Batista's army demoralised — only 300 rebels defeat 10,000 troops.
Dec 31, 1958Batista flees — after Che's forces capture Santa Clara (key rail hub). Batista escapes to Dominican Republic. Jan 8, 1959: Castro enters Havana in triumph.

Consolidating the Revolution (1959–1965)

▾
1959Agrarian Reform Law — Nationalised all landholdings over 400 hectares. 100,000 families received land. Directly targeted US sugar companies (United Fruit, Texaco).
1959–60Nationalisation wave — US refineries, utilities, banks nationalised. US imposes trade embargo (still in force). Cuba turns to USSR for oil, economic support.
1961Literacy Campaign — 100,000 "brigadistas" sent to rural Cuba to teach reading. Illiteracy fell from ~23% to ~3.9% in one year. Massive propaganda success; built genuine loyalty among rural poor.
Apr 1961Bay of Pigs — 1,400 CIA-trained Cuban exiles land at Playa Girón. Defeated in 72 hours. Catastrophic failure for Kennedy; dramatically strengthens Castro's domestic standing. CIA had assumed the Cuban people would rise up — they didn't.
1961Cuba declared socialist — Castro announces Marxist-Leninist course. CDRs (Committees for the Defence of the Revolution) established — neighbourhood surveillance, one per block. Single-party state takes form.
Oct 1962Cuban Missile Crisis — 13-day standoff. USSR places nuclear missiles in Cuba; Kennedy orders naval quarantine. Resolution: USSR removes missiles; US pledges not to invade Cuba; secretly removes Jupiter missiles from Turkey. Cuba was not consulted in the resolution — Khrushchev and Kennedy negotiated over Cuba's head. This angered Castro.
1965Communist Party of Cuba (PCC) becomes the sole legal party. Total political monopoly formalized. CDRs, UMAP labour camps for political enemies and "undesirables" (gay men, religious people).

Cuba in the Cold War (1965–1991)

▾
1967Che Guevara killed in Bolivia — Attempting to spread revolution to Latin America. CIA-trained Bolivian soldiers capture and execute him. Becomes the world's most recognizable revolutionary icon in death.
1975–88Cuba in Angola — 36,000 Cuban troops fight alongside MPLA government against US/South Africa-backed UNITA. Che's internationalism in practice. Nelson Mandela credited Cuba for tipping the balance against apartheid South Africa in southern Africa.
1976New Constitution — "Institutionalisation" of the revolution. Soviet-style structure. Cuba joins COMECON. Dependence on USSR sugar subsidies (~$4–6 billion/year at peak) deepens.
1980Mariel Boatlift — 125,000 Cubans leave in 5 months after Castro briefly allows emigration. Mix of political dissidents, economic migrants, and criminals Castro released from prisons. Embarrassment and relief simultaneously.
1991USSR dissolves — Cuba loses ~$5 billion annually in subsidies overnight. Special Period in Time of Peace (Período Especial): food rationing, blackouts, economic collapse. GDP contracted ~35% in 3 years.

Essay angles
Why did Castro succeed where so many Latin American revolutionaries failed?

Four factors: geography (Sierra Maestra mountains gave the guerrillas a base), Batista's brittleness (demoralised army, corrupt government), peasant support (land promises + treating rural people well), and media (Herbert Matthews NYT interview 1957 — legitimised the movement internationally). The 12-survivor story is especially powerful: argue Castro turned catastrophic failure into myth.

How did the Cold War shape Cuba's revolution?

US hostility drove Cuba toward the USSR — but was this inevitable? Argue: US embargo and Bay of Pigs forced the alignment (Castro was not initially committed to Marxism — his 1953 Moncada speech is Martí-nationalist, not Marxist). OR argue: Castro used Cold War cynically — Soviet subsidies funded the regime while allowing him to blame US imperialism for Cuba's problems. Both are defensible.

United States in the Cold War

1945 – 1991
Context: After WWII, the US emerged as a superpower — the world's largest economy, sole possessor of nuclear weapons, and global guarantor of a new liberal international order. The Soviet challenge threatened this vision. US Cold War strategy rested on one word: containment (George Kennan, 1946) — prevent Soviet expansion, don't attempt rollback. This simple doctrine was interpreted and reinterpreted by every president from Truman to Bush.

Truman & Eisenhower — Building Containment (1945–1961)

▾
1946Kennan's "Long Telegram" + "X Article" — Soviet expansion is inevitable; the US must "contain" it at every pressure point. Becomes the intellectual foundation of US Cold War strategy.
1947Truman Doctrine — "Support free peoples resisting subjugation." Greece and Turkey as first recipients. Transforms containment from a strategic idea into a global commitment: communist threat anywhere is a US concern everywhere.
1947Marshall Plan — $13 billion to rebuild Western Europe. Strategic logic: prosperous countries don't go communist. Western Europe's recovery attributed partly to this.
1948–49Berlin Blockade & Airlift — Soviets blockade West Berlin; Truman responds with 324-day airlift (2.3 million tons delivered). First direct US-Soviet confrontation. Sets the tone for Berlin as the Cold War's symbolic heart.
1949NATO founded + USSR tests atomic bomb + China falls to communists — 1949 is the year the Cold War truly begins to feel existential to American policymakers.
1950NSC-68 — Secret document: quadruple defence spending; militarise containment. Truman initially resisted — Korean War enabled it.
1950–53Korean War — First "hot" Cold War. UN forces (80% US) push North Korea back; China intervenes Nov 1950. War ends in stalemate at 38th parallel. 36,000 US dead. The "forgotten war" but crucial: established that containment required military force.
1950–54McCarthyism / Red Scare — Senator Joseph McCarthy claims 205 communists in State Department. No proof. "Are you now or have you ever been..." HUAC investigations. Hollywood blacklist. Shows domestic dimension of Cold War fear.
1953CIA overthrows Mossadegh (Iran) — Operation Ajax; restores Shah. First major covert intervention. Sets a template for US Cold War in the Global South: destabilise unfriendly governments, install pro-US leaders.
1954CIA overthrows Árbenz (Guatemala) — Operation PBSUCCESS. Árbenz redistributed land owned by United Fruit Company (UFC) — Eisenhower acted. Key case: was containment about communism or US economic interests? Both, inseparably.
1956Hungarian Revolution — Hungarians rise against Soviet control. US "liberation" rhetoric had implied support — but Eisenhower does nothing. 30,000 die. Exposes gap between "rollback" rhetoric and actual US policy.
1957Sputnik — USSR launches first satellite. Shock to US confidence. Leads to NASA, NSF, defense-oriented education spending. "Missile gap" emerges as political issue.
1961Eisenhower's Farewell Address — Warns against the "military-industrial complex." Notes that defence spending creates its own political constituency for continued Cold War. One of the most important speeches of the era.

Kennedy to Nixon — Crisis & Vietnam (1961–1974)

▾
1961Bay of Pigs + Berlin Wall — Cuban disaster (April); Berlin Wall built (August). Kennedy's first year: two humiliations. Wall actually stabilises Berlin — stops East German brain drain. Kennedy privately relieved but publicly had to condemn it.
1961Alliance for Progress — $20 billion for Latin American development over 10 years. Direct response to Cuba's appeal: offer an alternative to revolution. Mixed results — economic aid often went to elites; military juntas received most support.
Oct 1962Cuban Missile Crisis — 13 days. ExComm deliberations: military wanted airstrikes (Gen. Curtis LeMay); Kennedy chose blockade. Khrushchev backs down; missiles removed. Kennedy seen as winner. Classified documents later revealed: the US also removed Jupiter missiles from Turkey secretly. Both sides compromised.
1964Gulf of Tonkin incident + Resolution — Alleged North Vietnamese attack on USS Maddox (one was real, the second likely fabricated). Resolution gives LBJ blank check to use force in Vietnam without declaring war. Pivotal: escalates a limited conflict into a full war.
1965–68Vietnam escalation — Troops rise from 16,000 (1963) to 543,000 (1969). LBJ's "guns and butter" policy: Vietnam + Great Society = impossible to fund both. "We were wrong, terribly wrong" — Robert McNamara, decades later.
Jan 1968Tet Offensive — NVA/Viet Cong attack 100+ South Vietnamese cities simultaneously. Military failure for North Vietnam — but psychological disaster for LBJ. Showed US public had been misled about progress. LBJ declines to run for re-election.
1969–73Vietnamisation + Nixon Doctrine — Nixon: train South Vietnamese to fight their own war; US troops withdraw. But also: secretly bombs Cambodia (1969–70), invades Cambodia (1970) sparking domestic protests (Kent State — 4 students killed by National Guard).
1971Pentagon Papers leaked — Daniel Ellsberg leaks classified history of Vietnam decision-making. Shows presidents lied to Congress and the public for decades. Nixon's "plumbers" unit tries to discredit Ellsberg — leads eventually to Watergate.
1972Nixon to China + SALT I — Nixon (anti-communist) opens relations with China; signals Sino-Soviet split can be exploited. SALT I: first strategic arms limitation treaty. Détente — the high point of Cold War diplomacy.
1974Nixon resigns (Watergate) — The break-in was about domestic political surveillance, but it shattered public trust in government. Combined with Vietnam, creates a crisis of American political legitimacy that shapes everything that follows.

Ford, Carter, Reagan — Crisis & Triumph (1974–1991)

▾
Apr 1975Fall of Saigon — North Vietnam takes Saigon; last Americans evacuate by helicopter from embassy roof. US first military defeat. Profound national trauma. "Vietnam syndrome" shapes US reluctance to deploy troops for a decade.
1975Helsinki Accords — 35 nations recognise post-WWII borders; USSR gets legitimacy it wanted. But human rights provisions (Basket III) gave Eastern European dissidents an international framework. Unintended consequence for the USSR.
1977–81Carter — Human Rights Policy — Cuts military aid to rights-abusing allies (Chile, Argentina). Panama Canal Treaties 1977 — returns canal to Panama. Camp David Accords 1978 (Egypt-Israel peace). But: undermined by Iran hostage crisis (1979) and Afghanistan invasion.
1979Soviet invasion of Afghanistan — Carter calls it "the greatest threat to peace since WWII." Carter Doctrine: US will defend Persian Gulf. Arms and trains mujaheddin. Blowback: the mujaheddin becomes the Taliban and al-Qaeda in the 1990s.
1981–89Reagan Doctrine — US actively supports anti-communist guerrillas worldwide: Contras (Nicaragua), mujaheddin (Afghanistan), UNITA (Angola), Khmer Rouge (Cambodia). Reverses détente; renewed arms race.
1983Grenada invasion — US removes Cuban-backed government. Small island, huge symbolism: US willing to use direct force again after Vietnam syndrome.
1986Iran-Contra affair revealed — Reagan administration secretly sold arms to Iran (arms embargo) and used proceeds to fund Nicaraguan Contras (Congress had banned it). Oliver North as the operative. Biggest Reagan scandal. Shows extreme commitment to Contra cause.
1987INF Treaty (Reagan-Gorbachev) — Eliminated all intermediate-range nuclear missiles. First arms reduction (not just limitation) treaty. Signals the Cold War is ending.
Nov 9, 1989Berlin Wall falls — Symbol of the Cold War's end. Eastern Europe's revolutions follow. Germany reunifies 1990. USSR dissolves Dec 25, 1991.

Essay angles
Was containment a success?

Argue yes: the USSR collapsed, Western Europe remained free, no nuclear war occurred. But qualify: Vietnam showed containment's human cost and strategic bankruptcy in civil-war contexts. Korea ended in stalemate. Iran and Guatemala show containment was used to protect US economic interests, not just democracy. Orthodox view: containment worked. Revisionist: it caused enormous suffering in the Global South.

Why did the US fail in Vietnam?

Four-factor argument: Political (no clear endgame; search-and-destroy made enemies, not victories), Strategic (guerrilla war made conventional US superiority irrelevant), Domestic (public opinion turned after Tet; protests; credibility gap), Ideological (South Vietnam lacked legitimacy; Ho Chi Minh was a nationalist first, communist second — the US misread the conflict as Cold War rather than civil war/decolonisation).

Chile & Argentina

1970s – 1983
Context: Latin America in the Cold War became a battlefield between US-backed anti-communism and left-wing movements drawing on Marxism, Peronism, and liberation theology. Two countries exemplify this most starkly: Chile, where a democratically elected Marxist was overthrown by a US-backed military coup; and Argentina, where a military junta waged a secret war against its own people, disappearing 30,000.

Chile — Allende (1970–1973)

▾
1970Allende wins election — 36.3% in a 3-way race; Unidad Popular coalition. First democratically elected Marxist president in the world. Nixon: "I don't see why we need to stand by and let a country go communist due to the irresponsibility of its own people." CIA immediately begins Operation Track I (block his confirmation), then Track II (back a coup).
1971Nationalisation of copper — Unanimous congressional vote to nationalize Chile's copper mines (Anaconda, Kennecott). "Chile's salary." Allende calls it "the second independence." US companies furious.
1971Agrarian reform + social programs — 2.8M hectares redistributed; free milk for children; healthcare expanded. GDP grew 8% in 1971 — Allende's first year is economically successful.
1972Economic crisis begins — Inflation rises: 22% (1971) → 163% (1972) → 508% (1973). US blocks Chile from international loans (World Bank, IMF). ITT Corporation lobbies US government to destabilize Allende. Nixon/Kissinger: "make the economy scream."
Oct 1972"March of the Empty Pots" — Bourgeois women protest food shortages by banging empty pots. Truckers' strike (CIA-funded) paralyses the economy. Allende appoints military officers to cabinet — mistakes it for loyalty.

Chile — The Coup & Pinochet (Sept 11, 1973 – 1990)

▾
Sep 11, 1973Military coup — Generals Pinochet (army), Leigh (air force), Admiral Merino (navy), General Mendoza (carabineros) seize power. Air force bombs La Moneda palace. Allende dies inside — officially suicide, disputed. CIA involvement: Operation FUBELT; Kissinger approved destabilization. The date — Sept 11 — is significant in Chilean memory decades before 2001.
1973Immediate repression — National Stadium becomes detention camp; 40,000+ arrested in first weeks; 3,000–5,000 killed immediately. Victor Jara (folk singer) tortured and killed in stadium. Hands broken so he could not play guitar.
1974DINA established — Secret police under Manuel Contreras. Systematic torture, disappearances. Villa Grimaldi: main torture center in Santiago. "Caravan of Death": military officers execute ~70 prisoners across the country in the first weeks.
1975Operation Condor — Multinational network: Chile, Argentina, Brazil, Uruguay, Paraguay, Bolivia coordinate to hunt down dissidents internationally. CIA provides intelligence and logistical support. Targets even include exile communities in Europe.
1975–82Chicago Boys — neoliberal shock therapy — Economics graduates trained under Milton Friedman implement radical free market reform: privatizations, free trade, cut social spending. Inflation eventually controlled. Growth in late 1970s — the "Chilean miracle." But: inequality soars; 1982 economic crisis; poverty remains high for the poor.
1980Pinochet constitution — Institutionalises his rule; anti-communist clauses; binds future governments to neoliberal economic model. Partly still in force (modified). Required a 1988 plebiscite on his continued rule.
1988"No" campaign wins plebiscite — 55.99% vs 44.01%. Pinochet loses his own referendum. Returns Chile to democracy. Patricio Aylwin elected 1990. Transition is constrained — Pinochet stays as army chief until 1998.
1991Rettig Report (CNVR) — Documents 3,197 killed/disappeared. Human rights groups estimate higher. Pinochet never convicted — dies December 2006. 1998: Arrested in London on Spanish warrant (Judge Baltasar Garzón). Returned to Chile on health grounds; sets precedent for international human rights law.

Argentina — Perón, Dirty War & Transition (1946–1983)

▾
1946–55Juan Perón in power — "Justicialismo": populist nationalism mixing workers' rights with anti-communism and anti-US rhetoric. Eva Perón ("Evita") — women's suffrage (1947), social welfare programs, cult of personality. Perón ousted by military coup 1955.
1973Perón returns from 18 years exile — Re-elected president. But Peronism has split: left-wing Montoneros guerrillas and right-wing Triple A death squads both claim his legacy. Perón dies July 1974. Wife Isabel Perón (VP) becomes president — incompetent; economy collapses; terror accelerates.
Mar 24, 1976"El Proceso" coup — Military seizes power. Junta: General Videla (army), Admiral Massera (navy), Brigadier Agosti (air force). US Secretary of State Kissinger tells Videla (Jan 1976, months before coup): "If there are things that have to be done, you should do them quickly."
1976–83The Dirty War (Guerra Sucia) — 30,000 estimated disappeared (desaparecidos); official figure: 9,000; human rights consensus: ~30,000. "Subversion" defined broadly: guerrillas, trade unionists, teachers, journalists, students, priests, lawyers. Method: clandestine. No bodies, no charges, no accountability.
1976–83ESMA — Navy Mechanics School, Buenos Aires. Largest detention/torture center. Up to 5,000 prisoners passed through; most never returned. Survivors describe systematic torture, then "death flights" — drugged prisoners thrown alive into Río de la Plata or the Atlantic from military planes.
1976–83Theft of babies — Pregnant women had babies forcibly taken; mothers killed; babies given to military families. Abuelas de Plaza de Mayo (Grandmothers) have since located 100+ grandchildren through DNA testing. A uniquely horrifying dimension.
1977Madres de Plaza de Mayo — Mothers of the disappeared gather every Thursday in the plaza in front of the government palace, holding photos of their children. Regime calls them "Las Locas" (the crazy women). First organised public resistance. White headscarves as symbol. Become an international symbol of human rights courage.
Apr 1982Falklands War (Malvinas) — General Galtieri invades Falkland Islands to distract from economic crisis and boost nationalism. Miscalculated Thatcher's response and US support for Britain. Britain retakes islands by June 14; 649 Argentine soldiers dead, 255 British. Military humiliation collapses the junta.
1983Return to democracy — Raúl Alfonsín (Radical Party) elected. CONADEP Commission → "Nunca Más" ("Never Again") report (1984): documented 8,960 disappeared. 1985: Trial of the Juntas — Videla sentenced to life; Massera to life.
1987–90Punto Final + Due Obedience + Menem pardons — Under military pressure, Alfonsín limits prosecutions. Menem (1990) pardons junta leaders. But: 2003 — Kirchner government revokes pardon laws; full prosecutions resume. Videla dies in prison 2013.

Essay angles
How responsible was the US for the Chilean coup?

Directly involved — CIA funded El Mercurio newspaper, backed truckers' strikes, supplied intelligence to the military. Nixon personally authorized Operation FUBELT. Kissinger: "I don't see why we need to stand by..." But: avoid overstating US omnipotence — the Chilean military had its own motivations (fear of Marxism, institutional interests, class solidarity). The coup was Chilean-driven but US-enabled. Revisionist historians (Kornbluh) emphasise US culpability; orthodox (Rabe) note Chile's internal polarisation.

How did the Dirty War end?

Three intersecting causes: Falklands disaster (destroyed military's legitimacy — they couldn't even win a war against a declining power), economic collapse (hyperinflation, debt crisis undermined the regime's technocratic credibility), and human rights pressure (Madres, international NGOs, Carter's human rights policy cut military aid). Argue: the Madres are symbolically powerful but the Falklands delivered the decisive blow — without military humiliation, the transition might not have happened when it did.

Compare Chile and Argentina's authoritarian regimes

Similar: Both US-backed, anti-communist, used disappearances/torture, operated during the Cold War 1970s. Both ended through peaceful transition. Different: Chile had Pinochet (one man) vs. Argentina's rotating junta. Chile's repression targeted specific opposition; Argentina's Dirty War was broader, more chaotic. Chile implemented sustained neoliberal economics (Chicago Boys) with some success; Argentina's economics were disastrous. Chile transitioned through a plebiscite Pinochet called; Argentina's transition forced by Falklands humiliation.

Historiographical Perspectives

Use in every essay
How to use historiography in IB: Don't just name-drop. The formula is: Name the school → Define its core argument → Apply it with specific evidence → Qualify or challenge it. Aim for at least 2 perspectives per essay, and try to show they are in tension.
Orthodox / Traditional
The dominant view at the time or shortly after events. For the Cold War, orthodoxy holds that the USSR was the aggressor and the US was defending freedom. For civil rights, orthodoxy focuses on great leaders (MLK) and key legislation. Tends to be top-down, written by those close to power or from the "winner's" perspective.
Cold War: US as defender of freedom Civil Rights: MLK + legislation as the story Cuba: Castro as communist threat
Example: Orthodox Cold War historians (e.g. Arthur Schlesinger Jr.) argued Soviet aggression made containment not just justified but morally necessary. The USSR was expansionist; the US responded defensively.
Revisionist
Challenges the dominant narrative — usually more critical of power, more sympathetic to those who lost. For the Cold War, revisionists argue US economic imperialism was as much to blame as Soviet aggression (William Appleman Williams, Gar Alperovitz). For civil rights, revisionists question whether legislation addressed structural racism.
Cold War: US imperialism drove the conflict Civil Rights: legislation was insufficient Chile: US primarily responsible for coup Cuba: US embargo caused Cuba's problems
Example: Revisionist historians like William Appleman Williams ("The Tragedy of American Diplomacy," 1959) argued the US pursued an "Open Door" empire — economic expansion disguised as freedom. Guatemala (1954) and Iran (1953) were about protecting US corporate interests, not fighting communism.
Post-Revisionist
Synthesises orthodox and revisionist views — acknowledges both superpowers had responsibility for the Cold War, misperceptions drove decisions on both sides, and the conflict cannot be reduced to either US or Soviet aggression alone. More nuanced; uses recently declassified archives from both sides.
Cold War: shared responsibility Chile: US-enabled but Chilean-driven Cuban Missile Crisis: both sides miscalculated
Example: John Lewis Gaddis ("The Cold War: A New History," 2005) uses Soviet archives to show Stalin was genuinely expansionist — not just reactive. Both superpowers created a self-perpetuating spiral of insecurity. Post-revisionism is currently dominant in academic Cold War history.
Subaltern Perspective
Developed by Ranajit Guha and the Subaltern Studies collective (applied to Latin America by scholars like Walter Mignolo). Focuses on the experiences and agency of ordinary, marginalised people — peasants, women, workers, indigenous communities. Challenges both orthodox and revisionist histories for still focusing on elites (whether US/Soviet statesmen or revolutionary leaders). Asks: what did the revolution/movement mean for the people at the bottom?
Civil Rights: grassroots organisers, women Liberation Theology: CEB communities Cuba: rural peasants' own motivations Argentina: Madres as subaltern resistance
Example: A subaltern reading of the Cuban Revolution asks: what did the Sierra Maestra peasants actually want? Were they committed Marxists, or did they support Castro for land reform, protection from Batista's army, and immediate material gains? Their motivations may not match Castro's ideological narrative.
Grassroots Perspective
Closely related to the subaltern perspective but more focused on political organizing from below — how movements are built by ordinary people rather than charismatic leaders. Questions the "great man" (or great leader) model of history. Argues that leadership gets the credit for victories that were built by anonymous organisers, community networks, and local activists.
Civil Rights: Ella Baker, local NAACP chapters Liberation Theology: CEBs, not bishops Madres de Plaza de Mayo
Example: Grassroots historians of the Civil Rights Movement (Clayborne Carson, Charles Payne) argue that SNCC and local organisers in Mississippi — not MLK's speeches — were the movement's backbone. The Montgomery Boycott was organized by Jo Ann Robinson before Rosa Parks's arrest; MLK arrived after the movement was already in motion.
Quick comparison: how perspectives differ on key topics

Cold War — Orthodox vs Revisionist vs Post-revisionist

▾
  • Orthodox: Soviet aggression was the primary cause; US defence of freedom was justified; containment was a moral necessity
  • Revisionist: US economic imperialism drove the conflict; Open Door policy meant the US needed open markets globally; Stalin was responding to legitimate security fears after WWII
  • Post-revisionist: Both sides bear responsibility; shared misperceptions and security dilemmas; Soviet archives confirm expansionism but also genuine fear of US; the Cold War was a tragedy, not a morality play

Civil Rights — Liberal/Orthodox vs Grassroots/Subaltern

▾
  • Liberal/Orthodox: MLK, Supreme Court decisions, LBJ legislation — civil rights won through enlightened leadership and constitutional channels
  • Grassroots: Ella Baker, SNCC, local organisers, Jo Ann Robinson — the movement was built from below; MLK arrived to movements already in progress
  • Revisionist/Radical: The 1964–65 Acts were insufficient — they addressed legal equality but not economic justice; Black Power was the honest recognition of this failure

Liberation Theology — Traditional Catholic vs Progressive/Subaltern

▾
  • Traditional/Vatican: Liberation theology contaminated Christianity with Marxist class analysis; politicised the Church; undermined spiritual mission; rightly opposed
  • Progressive/Grassroots: Authentic response to Christ's command to serve the poor; CEBs empowered communities; liberation theology gave a voice and framework to people who had neither
  • Subaltern: What did the CEB participants themselves believe and want? Were they using the Church as a vehicle for their own pre-existing resistance? The theology may have facilitated, not created, grassroots activism
