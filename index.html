Paper 1

Rights and Protest

US Civil Rights Movement
1954 – 1965
United States
Context: Black Americans faced legal segregation under Jim Crow laws in the South. The 1896 Plessy v. Ferguson ruling had established "separate but equal." Post-WWII, Black veterans who fought for democracy abroad returned home to inequality — creating powerful moral pressure for change.
▶ Starting ▶ Maintaining momentum ▶ Legislative victory
How it started — legal challenges & early protests
1954Brown v. Board of Education: Supreme Court unanimously ruled racial segregation in schools unconstitutional — overturned Plessy v. Ferguson. NAACP lawyer Thurgood Marshall argued the case.
1955Murder of Emmett Till: 14-year-old Black boy tortured and murdered in Mississippi; his open casket funeral galvanized the nation. Killers acquitted by all-white jury.
1955–56Montgomery Bus Boycott: Rosa Parks arrested for refusing to give up her seat. MLK leads 381-day boycott. Supreme Court rules bus segregation unconstitutional. Launches MLK as national figure.
1957Little Rock Crisis: Arkansas Governor Faubus uses National Guard to block Black students at Central High School. Eisenhower sends federal troops. Tests federal will to enforce desegregation.
Escalation — direct action & media strategy
1960Greensboro Sit-ins: Four Black college students sit at a segregated Woolworth's lunch counter; refused service but stay. Spreads to 55 cities in weeks. SNCC founded.
1961Freedom Rides: CORE and SNCC members test desegregation on interstate buses across the South. Met with mob violence in Anniston and Birmingham. Attorney General Robert Kennedy forced to act.
1963Birmingham Campaign: MLK deliberately provokes Police Commissioner Bull Connor, who uses fire hoses and police dogs on peaceful protesters. Global media coverage forces JFK to act.
Aug 1963March on Washington: 250,000 marchers gather at Lincoln Memorial. MLK delivers "I Have a Dream" speech — the largest demonstration in US history at that point.
Sep 196316th Street Baptist Church bombing: KKK kills four Black girls (Addie Mae Collins, Cynthia Wesley, Carole Robertson, Carol Denise McNair). Shocks the nation.
Legislative victories
1964Civil Rights Act: Banned discrimination in public spaces and employment on basis of race, colour, religion, sex, national origin. Signed by LBJ after JFK's assassination.
Mar 1965"Bloody Sunday" / Selma: State troopers beat marchers on the Edmund Pettus Bridge. Televised footage of brutality prompts LBJ to address Congress. John Lewis suffered fractured skull.
1965Voting Rights Act: Outlawed discriminatory voting practices (literacy tests, poll taxes). Within 2 years, Black voter registration in Mississippi rose from 6.7% to 59.8%.

Historiographical Perspectives
Top-down / Liberal: Focuses on MLK, Supreme Court decisions, LBJ legislation — civil rights as achieved through elite leadership and legal reform.
Grassroots / Subaltern: Emphasises local organizers, women (Ella Baker, Jo Ann Robinson), NAACP chapters — argues the movement was won from below, not by charismatic leaders alone.
Revisionist: Questions the limits of the 1964–65 legislation; highlights systemic economic inequality that laws didn't address; elevates Malcolm X and Black Power as responses to nonviolence's limits.
Apartheid South Africa
1948 – 1994
South Africa
Context: Dutch (Boer) settlers arrived 1652; British colonized 1806. The 1910 Union of South Africa had long enforced racial discrimination (pass laws, land acts). In 1948, the Afrikaner National Party won elections on an explicit apartheid ("separateness") platform.
How it was built — apartheid legislation
1948National Party wins election: Afrikaner nationalists implement apartheid system. D.F. Malan becomes PM.
1950Population Registration Act: Every person classified by race (White, Coloured, Indian, Bantu/Black). Race determined all aspects of life.
1950Group Areas Act: Forced racial segregation of residential and business areas. Black communities bulldozed (e.g., Sophiatown, 1955).
1950Suppression of Communism Act: Broadly defined "communism" to ban any opposition to apartheid.
1953Bantu Education Act: Minister Verwoerd designed deliberately inferior education for Black Africans — "to train them for manual labour only." Removed Black schools from churches to state control.
1953Pass Laws enforced: Black South Africans required to carry "passbooks" at all times; restricted movement into white areas.
Resistance — from protest to armed struggle
1952Defiance Campaign: ANC and SAIC organise nonviolent resistance against unjust laws. 8,500 volunteers deliberately break apartheid laws; arrested. ANC membership surges from 7,000 to 100,000.
1955Freedom Charter: Congress Alliance declares "South Africa belongs to all who live in it." Blueprint for post-apartheid society.
1956Women's March on Pretoria: 20,000 women of all races protest pass laws. "You have struck a woman, you have struck a rock."
Mar 1960Sharpeville Massacre: PAC anti-pass protest; police open fire on crowd, killing 69 (most shot in the back). International outrage. ANC and PAC banned. Turning point — Mandela concludes nonviolence was insufficient.
1961ANC forms MK (Umkhonto we Sizwe): Armed wing — "Spear of the Nation." Mandela as commander. Targeted infrastructure sabotage (not people).
1964Rivonia Trial: Mandela and 7 others sentenced to life imprisonment. Mandela's speech: "An ideal for which I am prepared to die." Imprisoned on Robben Island for 27 years.
1976Soweto Uprising: Students protest mandatory teaching in Afrikaans. Police kill Hector Pieterson (13 years old). Over 600 die. Galvanises global anti-apartheid movement.
1977Steve Biko killed: Black Consciousness Movement leader dies in police custody. Leads to international condemnation and UN arms embargo.
1985–86State of Emergency: P.W. Botha declares emergency. Intensifying sanctions from US (Comprehensive Anti-Apartheid Act, 1986), UK, and globally.
1990Mandela released (Feb 11) by F.W. de Klerk. ANC/PAC unbanned. Negotiations for democratic transition begin.
1994First democratic election (April 27). ANC wins 62%. Mandela inaugurated as South Africa's first Black president. Apartheid ends.

Key Laws to Know
  • Population Registration Act (1950): Racial classification of all people
  • Group Areas Act (1950): Forced residential racial segregation
  • Bantu Education Act (1953): Deliberately inferior Black education ("train for servitude")
  • Pass Laws: Required passbooks; controlled Black movement; arrests without cause
  • Suppression of Communism Act (1950): Banned any anti-apartheid dissent broadly
Historiographical Perspectives
Liberal: Emphasises individual rights and the moral campaign — role of international pressure, Mandela as symbol of universal rights.
Radical / Marxist: Apartheid as "racial capitalism" — the system existed to exploit cheap Black labour for white mining and agricultural interests.
Subaltern / Grassroots: Focus on township resistance, women's activism (Federation of South African Women), trade unions (COSATU, 1985) — ordinary people as agents of change.
Paper 2

Independence Movements

India — Independence Movement
1885 – 1947
India
Context: British rule (Raj) from 1858 (after 1857 Sepoy Mutiny). Economic exploitation: India forced to buy British manufactured goods while raw materials were extracted — "drain of wealth" theory. 300 million people governed by ~1,000 ICS officers. Indian National Congress founded 1885 by educated elite.
How it started — early nationalism (1885–1919)
1885Indian National Congress (INC) founded: Initially moderate; petitioned British for reforms. Dominated by English-educated elite.
1905Partition of Bengal: Viceroy Curzon splits Bengal on religious lines. Sparks mass Swadeshi movement — boycott of British goods; promotion of Indian-made products.
1915Gandhi returns from South Africa: Brings satyagraha (nonviolent resistance, "truth-force") philosophy. Transforms the independence movement into a mass campaign.
1919Rowlatt Act: Allowed 2-year imprisonment without trial for anyone suspected of terrorism. Deeply unpopular; united Indian opinion against British rule.
Apr 1919Amritsar Massacre (Jallianwala Bagh): General Dyer orders troops to fire on unarmed civilians in an enclosed garden. At least 379 killed (Indian estimates much higher). No evacuation permitted. Radicalises Indian politics permanently — Nehru wrote it shook his faith in the British.
Mass mobilisation — Gandhi's campaigns (1920–1942)
1920–22Non-Cooperation Movement: Gandhi calls for boycott of British goods, courts, and schools. First mass movement — crosses class, region, religion. Called off after Chauri Chaura violence (1922).
1930Salt March (Dandi March): Gandhi walks 240 miles (26 days) to the sea to make salt illegally, defying British salt monopoly. 60,000 people arrested nationwide. First global media event for Indian independence.
1930–31Civil Disobedience Movement: Mass refusal to obey unjust laws. Gandhi–Irwin Pact (1931) — Gandhi calls off campaign; invited to Round Table Conference in London.
1935Government of India Act: Provided provincial self-governance. INC wins elections in 8 of 11 provinces, demonstrating democratic legitimacy.
1942Quit India Movement: Gandhi's "Do or Die" speech. British immediately arrest Gandhi, Nehru, and all INC leadership. 100,000+ arrested. Shows Britain's weakening grip — requires 57 battalions to suppress.
Independence & aftermath (1945–1947+)
1945–46Post-WWII: Britain exhausted. INA trials (Bose's Indian National Army) — prosecutions backfire; INA soldiers seen as heroes. Royal Indian Navy mutiny 1946.
Aug 1947Independence and Partition: India and Pakistan created. Up to 2 million killed in communal violence; 14 million displaced. Gandhi assassinated January 1948.
1947–64Nehru's India: Five-Year Plans for industrialisation; mixed economy; Non-Aligned Movement (NAM) in Cold War; secularism in constitution; founding of IITs.
Mahatma Gandhi
Satyagraha; mass mobilisation; "Father of the Nation"
Jawaharlal Nehru
First PM; Five-Year Plans; Non-Aligned Movement
Muhammad Ali Jinnah
Muslim League; demanded and achieved Pakistan
B.R. Ambedkar
Dalit rights activist; wrote Indian Constitution
Subhas Chandra Bose

General Dyer
Ordered Amritsar Massacre; symbol of British brutality
Key Methods
  • Satyagraha: Nonviolent resistance / "truth-force" — moral pressure over violence
  • Civil disobedience: Deliberately breaking unjust laws; accepting arrest
  • Economic boycotts: Swadeshi — home-spun khadi cloth symbolises resistance
  • Mass mobilisation: Cross-caste, cross-class, cross-religion coalitions
  • Legal challenges: INC used British constitutional structures
Historiographical Perspectives
Nationalist: Congress-led; Gandhi and Nehru as heroes; independence as moral victory of nonviolence.
Subaltern (Ranajit Guha, Partha Chatterjee): Peasant revolts, women, lower castes had their own autonomous resistance — not simply followers of elite Congress leadership. Guha's "Subaltern Studies" project.
Revisionist: Partition as catastrophic failure of Congress/Gandhi leadership; Ambedkar's critique: Gandhi's campaigns didn't address caste oppression; Bose shows limits of nonviolence.
Imperial / British: "Winds of change" — independence inevitable after WWII; Britain's "orderly transfer of power." Critics: ignores decades of brutal suppression.
Cuba — Independence from Spain / US Domination
1868 – 1902 (and beyond)
Cuba
Context: Cuba was Spain's richest remaining colony — a sugar monoculture economy worked initially by enslaved Africans. Spain imposed heavy trade restrictions, high taxes, and excluded Cuban-born Creoles from political power. The 1791 Haitian Revolution demonstrated that freedom was possible, inspiring Afro-Cuban and Creole nationalists alike. Deep social inequality between peninsulares, Creoles, freed Blacks, and slaves.
How it started — independence wars (1868–1898)
1868Ten Years War begins: Carlos Manuel de Céspedes frees his slaves and declares independence — "Grito de Yara." Long guerrilla war; 200,000 dead. Ends in compromise (Zanjón Pact, 1878) — no independence; but slavery abolished 1886.
1892José Martí founds the Cuban Revolutionary Party (PRC) in exile in New York. Builds a broad coalition uniting Cubans across race and class. His writings ("Our America") define Cuban national identity.
Feb 1895War of Independence begins. Martí returns to Cuba from exile and is killed at Dos Ríos (May 19, 1895). Becomes a martyr and the most powerful symbol of Cuban independence — "The Apostle."
1895–98Spanish reconcentration camps: General Weyler herds 400,000 civilians into camps to deny guerrillas support. Up to 100,000 die of disease and starvation. Creates international outrage.
1898USS Maine explosion in Havana harbour (Feb 15). US declares war on Spain ("Remember the Maine!"). Spanish-American War — Spain defeated in months. Cuba "liberated" but now occupied by the US.
Neo-colonialism — US domination (1898–1952)
1901Platt Amendment: Written into Cuban constitution by the US. Gave the US the right to intervene militarily in Cuban affairs, veto treaties, and permanently lease Guantánamo Bay. Cuba "independent" in name only.
1902Formal independence: But US companies own most of the sugar industry, railroads, and utilities. Martí's vision of "Cuba for Cubans" unfulfilled.
1933Sergeant Batista's revolt: Fulgencio Batista rises during political chaos; becomes the key power broker in Cuba. Platt Amendment repealed (1934) but US economic dominance continues.
1952Batista's coup: Cancels elections when polls show he will lose. Establishes brutal, corrupt dictatorship backed by the US and organised crime. Cuba ripe for revolution.
José Martí
Poet, writer, "The Apostle"; ideological father of independence; died 1895
Antonio Maceo
Afro-Cuban military leader; "Bronze Titan"; killed 1896
Máximo Gómez
Military commander of independence forces
Fulgencio Batista
Dictator 1952–59; US-backed; corrupt; corrupt
Key Themes for Essays
  • Sugar monoculture: Cuba's economy made it dependent on a single crop — vulnerability to price changes, foreign control
  • US neo-colonialism: Platt Amendment = formal control; economic domination = informal control
  • Martí's legacy: His ideas about race, class, and national unity became the ideological foundation Castro later claimed
  • Haitian Revolution influence: Showed enslaved people could win freedom; inspired Cuban Afro-descendants
  • Social inequality: Extreme gap between landowning elite and landless workers made the island explosive
Historiographical Perspectives
Cuban Nationalist: Independence as heroic struggle; Martí as visionary; US intervention as betrayal of Cuban sovereignty.
US / Imperial: US "liberated" Cuba from Spain; Monroe Doctrine justified protection of hemisphere. Critics: economic exploitation replaced colonial rule.
Marxist / Dependency theory: Cuba moved from Spanish colonialism to US neo-colonialism without true independence; Platt Amendment proof that formal independence meant nothing without economic sovereignty.
Paper 2

Authoritarian States

Nazi Germany — Hitler
1919 – 1945
Germany
Context: Germany humiliated by the Treaty of Versailles (1919): £6,600 million in reparations, "war guilt" clause (Article 231), loss of 13% territory, 6 million Germans placed under foreign rule. Weimar Republic: democratic but fragile — Article 48 allowed emergency decrees. Hyperinflation 1923 wiped out savings. Great Depression 1929 caused 6 million unemployed by 1932.
How Hitler rose to power (1919–1934)
1919Hitler joins DAP (German Workers' Party). Within months becomes its propaganda chief. Renamed NSDAP (Nazis) 1920 with 25-point programme.
Nov 1923Beer Hall Putsch: Hitler's failed coup in Munich. Arrested and sentenced to 5 years (served 9 months). In Landsberg Prison, dictates Mein Kampf — outlines racial ideology and plans for expansion.
1929–32Great Depression: Nazi vote surges from 2.6% (1928) to 37.4% (July 1932). Scapegoated Jews and communists for Germany's problems. Mass unemployment made radical solutions appealing.
Jan 30, 1933Hitler appointed Chancellor by President Hindenburg. Hindenburg thought he could control Hitler — miscalculation that changed history.
Feb 27, 1933Reichstag Fire: Parliament building burns. Blamed on communist Marinus van der Lubbe. Emergency Decree (Feb 28) suspends civil liberties, allows mass arrest of communists.
Mar 23, 1933Enabling Act: Gave Hitler power to rule by decree for 4 years, bypassing Reichstag. Passed 444–84 (only SPD voted against). Key constitutional step — Hitler used legal framework to destroy democracy.
Jun 1934Night of the Long Knives: Hitler orders the murder of SA leader Ernst Röhm and ~85 political rivals. Secures army's loyalty (army had feared SA rivals). Consolidates absolute power.
Aug 1934Hindenburg dies. Hitler merges roles of Chancellor and President — becomes Führer. Army swears personal oath to Hitler (not Germany). Total power achieved.
How the regime was maintained (1934–1939)
1933–34Coordination (Gleichschaltung): All institutions — trade unions, political parties, press, education — brought under Nazi control or abolished.
1933Goebbels' Propaganda Ministry: Controls all media — radio (70% of German homes had radios by 1939), cinema (Riefenstahl's Triumph of the Will, 1935), posters, rallies (Nuremberg). Created the Volksempfänger — cheap "People's Receiver" radio.
1935Nuremberg Laws: Jews stripped of German citizenship. Marriage and sexual relations between Jews and Germans banned. Jews cannot employ German women under 45. Legal foundation for persecution.
1936Hitler Youth made compulsory (8 million members by 1939). Indoctrination from childhood. Separate organisations for boys (combat training) and girls (motherhood preparation).
1936Remilitarisation of Rhineland: Violates Versailles; Britain and France do nothing. Proves Hitler's "calculated risk" strategy works. Builds popular support inside Germany.
Nov 1938Kristallnacht ("Night of Broken Glass"): 7,500 Jewish businesses destroyed, 1,400 synagogues burned, 91 Jews killed, 30,000 sent to concentration camps. State-organised pogrom.
War, Holocaust, and end (1939–1945)
Sep 1939Invasion of Poland: WWII begins. Rapid conquests (France 1940, Balkans 1941) boost Hitler's prestige inside Germany.
1941Operation Barbarossa: Invasion of USSR. Einsatzgruppen (mobile killing units) murder ~1.5 million Jews and others in mass shootings. Holocaust escalates.
Jan 1942Wannsee Conference: Senior Nazis coordinate the "Final Solution" — systematic extermination of European Jews. Death camps (Auschwitz, Treblinka, etc.) operational by 1942. 6 million Jews murdered.
Apr 30, 1945Hitler dies by suicide in Berlin bunker as Soviet forces close in. Germany surrenders May 8, 1945.

Methods of Control — the Five Pillars
  • Terror: SS, Gestapo (secret police), concentration camps — fear kept dissent silent
  • Propaganda: Goebbels controlled all media; radio, film, rallies, posters — manufactured consent
  • Youth indoctrination: Hitler Youth and League of German Girls from age 10 — created loyal generation
  • Economic recovery: Autobahns, rearmament cut unemployment from 6M to near-zero (1933–36) — bought popular support
  • Legal façade: Used Weimar constitution (Article 48, Enabling Act) to destroy democracy legally — gave legitimacy
Historiographical Perspectives
Intentionalism (Dawidowicz, Trevor-Roper): Hitler had a clear master plan — Mein Kampf spelled out his goals. The Holocaust was always his intention. Leader's will drove everything.
Functionalism / Structuralism (Broszat, Mommsen): The Nazi state was chaotic and polycratic — competing agencies without clear command. The Holocaust evolved gradually from radicalization of policy, not a single Führer-order.
Synthesis (Ian Kershaw): "Working towards the Führer" — officials anticipated Hitler's wishes and competed to please him, without direct orders. Combines structure and intent. Most influential current view.
Goldhagen ("Hitler's Willing Executioners"): Ordinary Germans were "eliminationist antisemites" who supported the Holocaust. Highly controversial — critics say it essentialises all Germans.
Cuba — Castro's Authoritarian State
1959 – present
Cuba
Context: By 1952, US companies owned 40% of Cuban sugar, most utilities, and telecommunications. Batista's regime was marked by gambling, corruption, and brutal political repression — while 1 in 3 Cubans lived in extreme poverty. 10,000+ political opponents were tortured or killed under Batista. The conditions made revolution inevitable.
How Castro came to power (1952–1959)
1952Batista's coup: Cancels elections. Castro (then a lawyer) challenges it legally — fails. Concludes that Batista can only be removed by force.
Jul 26, 1953Moncada Barracks attack (Santiago de Cuba): Castro leads 135 rebels in attack on military barracks — fails; 60+ rebels killed or captured. At trial, Castro delivers "History Will Absolve Me" speech — becomes the manifesto of the revolution.
1955Amnesty and exile: Castro released in amnesty; goes to Mexico. Meets Che Guevara — Argentine doctor and committed Marxist. Forms M-26-7 (July 26 Movement).
Dec 1956Granma landing: 82 men land in eastern Cuba. Batista's forces attack immediately; only 12 survive (including Fidel, Raúl Castro, Che Guevara). Retreat to Sierra Maestra mountains.
1957–58Sierra Maestra guerrilla war: Rebels win hearts of rural peasants; gain recruits. Urban underground (M-26-7) sabotages Batista in cities. Batista's forces brutal but demoralised. Frank País organises urban networks until his assassination (1957).
Dec 31, 1958Batista flees: After Che captures Santa Clara (key city). Batista escapes to Dominican Republic on New Year's Eve.
Jan 8, 1959Castro enters Havana in triumph. Revolution succeeds after just 3 years of guerrilla warfare.
How the regime was consolidated (1959–1970s)
1959Agrarian Reform Law: Nationalised all landholdings over 400 hectares. Distributed land to peasants. Directly hit US sugar interests.
1960Nationalisation wave: US-owned refineries, utilities, and companies nationalised. US imposes trade embargo (still in force today). Cuba turns to USSR for support.
1961Literacy Campaign: 100,000 student "brigadistas" sent to rural areas to teach reading. Illiteracy reduced from ~23% to ~3.9% in one year. Powerful tool for mobilisation and loyalty-building.
Apr 1961Bay of Pigs: 1,400 CIA-backed Cuban exiles attempt invasion — defeated in 72 hours. Catastrophic failure. Dramatically strengthens Castro's domestic and international standing.
1961Cuba declared socialist. Castro announces Marxist-Leninist course. Single-party state — dissent is suppressed. Political opponents imprisoned; UMAP labour camps for "undesirables."
Oct 1962Cuban Missile Crisis: USSR places nuclear missiles in Cuba. 13-day standoff between Kennedy and Khrushchev — closest the world came to nuclear war. Resolved: USSR removes missiles; US pledges not to invade Cuba.
1965Communist Party of Cuba (PCC) becomes the sole legal party. Total political monopoly formalised.
1976New Constitution: "Institutionalisation" of the revolution — Soviet-style structure. Cuba joins COMECON (Soviet economic bloc).

Methods of Control — Castro's Cuba
  • CDRs (Committees for Defence of the Revolution, 1960): Neighbourhood surveillance committees — every block had one; reported dissent
  • Cult of personality: Castro's marathon speeches (often 4+ hours), propaganda, image as revolutionary hero
  • Education and healthcare: Free universal healthcare and education — bought genuine loyalty from poor and rural Cubans
  • Economic control: State owns all major enterprises; ration book (libreta) system ensures dependence on state
  • Repression: Political prisoners; UMAP labour camps; exile of dissidents (1 million Cubans fled to US)
  • Cold War alignment: Soviet economic subsidies (~$4–6 billion/year at peak) kept Cuba afloat
Social Achievements (argue for Castro)
  • Literacy: Cuba went from ~23% illiteracy to one of the highest literacy rates in Latin America (99%+)
  • Healthcare: Infant mortality rate dropped from 60 per 1,000 to 10 per 1,000 by 1985; life expectancy rivalled developed nations
  • Land reform: Redistributed land to 100,000+ families who had been landless labourers
  • Education: University access massively expanded; free at all levels
Historiographical Perspectives
Revolutionary / Pro-Castro: Castro liberated Cubans from US neo-colonialism and domestic inequality. Social achievements in health and education represent real gains for the poor.
Liberal / Western: One-party state; suppression of free speech, press, religion; political prisoners. Social gains do not justify authoritarian control.
Cold War lens: Cuba as Soviet satellite (critics) vs. Cuba as genuinely non-aligned experiment (defenders). US hostility forced Cuba toward USSR.
Dependency theory: Castro broke the cycle of US economic exploitation. Cuban revolution as the only complete break from neo-colonialism in Latin America.
Both Papers

Historiographical Perspectives

Naming and explaining a historiographical perspective in your IB essay significantly strengthens your argument. Below are the key schools — with specific uses for each topic.

Intentionalism
The belief that a leader had a clear, pre-formed plan or ideology that drove their actions from the beginning. Their personal will and intentions are the primary explanation for events.
Hitler (Dawidowicz, Trevor-Roper) Castro: "History Will Absolve Me" as plan
Key example: Intentionalists argue Hitler's antisemitism was explicit in Mein Kampf (1925) — the Holocaust was always the destination, not an improvised outcome.
Functionalism / Structuralism
Argues that outcomes were shaped by the structures, institutions, and circumstances of the regime rather than a leader's master plan. Policy evolved through improvisation, competing bureaucracies, and situational pressures.
Hitler (Broszat, Mommsen — polycratic state) Castro's revolution as responding to circumstances
Key example: Functionalists argue the Holocaust emerged through a "cumulative radicalization" — no single Hitler order; competing Nazi agencies escalated persecution step by step.
Synthesis (Ian Kershaw)
"Working towards the Führer" — officials anticipated Hitler's ideological wishes and competed to implement them without explicit orders. Combines intent (Hitler's worldview) and structure (how it was actually executed).
Hitler — most accepted current view
Key quote to paraphrase: Kershaw describes a regime that was "cumulative radicalisation" driven by Hitler's ideological vision but executed through polycratic structures.
Subaltern / Grassroots Perspective
Developed by Ranajit Guha and the Subaltern Studies collective. Focuses on the experiences and agency of ordinary, marginalised people — peasants, women, workers — rather than elite leaders. Argues that "history from below" reveals a different story than official nationalist narratives.
India (Guha, Chatterjee) South Africa (townships, trade unions) US Civil Rights (local organisers) Cuba (rural peasants, M-26-7 base)
Key example (India): Guha argues Gandhi's Non-Cooperation Movement obscures the autonomous peasant revolts (e.g., Mappila Rebellion 1921) that weren't led by Congress elites at all.
Liberal / Top-Down Perspective
Focuses on great individuals, legal-constitutional change, and gradual reform within existing structures. Rights are won through legal challenges, legislation, and charismatic leadership.
US Civil Rights (MLK, Supreme Court, LBJ) South Africa (Mandela, de Klerk negotiations) India (Gandhi, Nehru, Congress leadership)
Marxist / Radical / Dependency Theory
Emphasises class conflict, economic exploitation, and systemic inequality. Apartheid, colonialism, and US domination of Cuba are explained as forms of economic exploitation, not just racial or political systems.
Apartheid as racial capitalism (Harold Wolpe) Cuba: US neo-colonialism; Castro as liberator India: drain of wealth theory (Dadabhai Naoroji)
Revisionist Perspectives
Challenges the dominant narrative — often questions whether the "official story" of a movement or regime is accurate, complete, or fair.
Goldhagen on Holocaust (ordinary Germans) India: Partition as failure; Ambedkar on Gandhi Cuba: Castro as authoritarian despite social gains
Exam Strategy

Tips for IB HL History

The three IB questions: Start, Maintain, End
Most Paper 2 questions ask one of three things: How did X rise to power / How did X maintain control / Why did X end (or fail). Structure every essay around ONE of these timeframes — don't try to cover all three.
Use historiography as a tool, not a name-drop.
Don't just name Intentionalism — explain what it means and apply it: "Intentionalist historians like Dawidowicz argue Hitler's racial ideology, outlined in Mein Kampf, made the Holocaust an inevitable outcome rather than a product of circumstance — this is supported by the deliberate escalation from Nuremberg Laws (1935) to extermination (1941–42)."
Cross-topic perspectives save time.
The subaltern perspective works for India (Guha), South Africa (township resistance), and US Civil Rights (grassroots organisers). Learn one perspective deeply and deploy it across multiple questions.
Cuba works for BOTH Paper 2 topics.
Castro's Cuba is both an independence movement (from Spain/US domination — Martí's legacy) AND an authoritarian state. In independence essays, emphasise Martí, sugar dependency, Platt Amendment. In authoritarian essays, emphasise Moncada, Sierra Maestra, CDRs, Bay of Pigs.
Specific facts beat generalisations every time.
"The British were repressive" = weak. "The Rowlatt Act (1919) allowed imprisonment without trial for 2 years on suspicion of terrorism alone, applied retroactively — this united Indians across class and religion against British rule" = strong. Always anchor claims in a specific event, date, law, or statistic.
Causation language for essays.
"This contributed to..." / "A more important factor was..." / "This alone cannot explain...because..." / "Intentionalist historians would argue X, however the functionalist counter-argument suggests..." These phrases signal historical thinking to examiners.
