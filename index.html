Essay Structure

The blueprint for every Paper 1 answer

📐 Standard Essay Template

Introduction

Define the key term(s) in the question. Link your definition to the prompt. Mention the study/studies you will use.

Body 1 (always)

State the study's Aim → Type → Procedure → Participants (ATPP). Connect it to the question.

Body 2 ("one or more")

Second study using the same ATPP structure. Used only when the question says "one or more" or "discuss."

Conclusion

Results of study/studies → Strength → Weakness → Validity / Generalizability → link back to prompt.

💡 ATPP Study Breakdown

A — Aim

"The aim of the study was to investigate whether…" — state the purpose.

T — Type

Lab experiment / Field study / Case study / Quasi-experiment / Survey / Correlation…

P — Procedure

What the researchers actually did. Keep it concise — 2-3 sentences max.

P — Participants

Who / How many / Any notable characteristics (age, gender, occupation, etc.)

📌 Exam Tips
⚡
"Explain" = 1 study minimum — but always name your study in the intro to signal you know it.8–10 marks
⚡
"Discuss" or "one or more" — use two studies; evaluate both in the conclusion.22 marks
⚡
Always link back to the exact wording of the question at least twice — in body and conclusion.
⚡
Ethics in conclusion — mention if the study was deceptive, if debriefing occurred, or if animals were used.
⚡
Multi-use studies — learn Maguire 2000, Loftus & Palmer 1974, and Tajfel 1971 cold — they appear everywhere.
⚡
Generalizability — consider sample size, WEIRD bias (Western, Educated, Industrial, Rich, Democratic).

Biological Approach

Brain, genes, hormones, neurotransmitters, pheromones, evolution, neuroplasticity

🌟 Priority Studies
Maguire et al
2000 · London Taxi Drivers · MRI Study
Neuroplasticity Localization Technique
⭐
AimTo see if navigational experience changes hippocampal structure
TypeQuasi-experiment / MRI brain imaging
Participants16 male London taxi drivers (2+ yrs exp.) vs. 50 controls
ProcedureMRI scans compared hippocampal volume of taxi drivers vs. non-taxi drivers
ResultsTaxi drivers had significantly larger posterior hippocampus; size correlated with years of experience
Memory Hook"More miles = bigger memory area." Taxi drivers grow their GPS brain region.
✅ StrengthObjective MRI data; real-world sample with genuine expertise
❌ WeaknessAll-male sample; causation unclear (did taxi work cause it, or did larger hippocampi attract taxi drivers?)
Draganski et al
2004 · Juggling Study · MRI
Neuroplasticity Technique
AimTo investigate whether learning a new skill changes grey matter in the brain
TypeTrue experiment with repeated measures; MRI used
Participants24 adults — 12 trained to juggle, 12 controls (no juggling)
ProcedureBrain scanned before training, after 3 months (when juggling mastered), and after another 3 months of no juggling
ResultsGrey matter increased in mid-temporal area when juggling was learned; decreased again when they stopped
Memory Hook"Use it or lose it." Brain physically grows with new skills, then shrinks without practice.
✅ StrengthTrue experiment — controls for individual differences; uses objective MRI measures
❌ WeaknessSmall sample (24); lab setting may not reflect real-world learning
Sharot et al
2007 · 9/11 Flashbulb Memory · fMRI
Technique (fMRI) Emotion & Memory
⭐
AimTo explore the neural basis of flashbulb memories vs. ordinary memories
TypeQuasi-experiment using fMRI brain scanning
Participants24 adults who were in NYC on 9/11; vs. those who were far away
ProcedureParticipants recalled 9/11 and other summer events while in fMRI scanner; brain activation compared
Results9/11 memories showed greater amygdala activation in those close to the event — amygdala links to emotional memory
Memory Hook"Amygdala = emotional alarm bell." High emotion → stronger, more vivid memory encoding.
✅ StrengthfMRI provides direct evidence of brain activity; links biology to cognitive process
❌ WeaknessQuasi-experiment — no random assignment; small sample; NYC context may limit generalizability
Rogers & Kesner
2003 · Acetylcholine in Rats · Animal Study
Neurotransmitters
AimTo investigate the role of acetylcholine on spatial memory in rats
TypeTrue lab experiment (animal research)
ParticipantsRats injected with either scopolamine (ACh blocker) or saline (control)
ProcedureRats navigated a maze; errors and timing were recorded
ResultsRats with blocked ACh made significantly more errors — showing ACh is vital for spatial memory
Memory Hook"Block ACh → bad at mazes." Acetylcholine = the memory chemical.
✅ StrengthHigh control over variables; causal relationship established
❌ WeaknessAnimal research — ethical concerns; cannot fully generalize to humans
Newcomer et al
1999 · Cortisol & Memory · Hormones
Hormones
AimTo test whether elevated cortisol (stress hormone) impairs verbal memory
TypeDouble-blind true experiment
Participants51 participants — 3 groups: high cortisol, low cortisol, placebo
ProcedureParticipants took cortisol tablets for 4 days then completed verbal memory tests
ResultsHigh cortisol group performed significantly worse on memory tasks
Memory Hook"Stress kills memory." High cortisol = forgetting more.
✅ StrengthDouble-blind design eliminates experimenter and participant bias
❌ WeaknessArtificial cortisol administration ≠ natural stress; short duration (4 days)
Wedekind et al
1995 · Sweaty T-Shirt Study · Pheromones
Pheromones Evolution
AimTo test whether humans use smell (MHC genes) to select genetically compatible mates
TypeLab experiment
Participants44 women, 49 men — MHC genotypes tested
ProcedureMen wore t-shirts for 2 days; women smelled the shirts and rated attractiveness. MHC similarity compared.
ResultsWomen preferred smell of men with dissimilar MHC genes (greater immune diversity benefit)
Memory Hook"Smell the opposite immune system." MHC dissimilarity = healthier offspring.
✅ StrengthControlled procedure; clear evolutionary rationale
❌ WeaknessArtificial setting; oral contraceptives reversed preference (ethical and methodological concern)
Kendler et al
2006 · Twin Study · Genetic Similarity
Genetics Twin Studies
⭐
AimTo examine genetic vs. environmental factors in major depression using twins
TypeLongitudinal twin study (concordance rates)
Participants~42,000 twin pairs from the Swedish Twin Registry
ProcedureCompared MZ (identical) and DZ (fraternal) twins for rates of depression. MZ share 100% DNA; DZ share ~50%.
ResultsMZ twins had higher concordance rates (~38%) than DZ twins (~26%) — suggesting genetic influence on depression
Memory Hook"Identical twins, more similar depression risk." Genetics plays a role but isn't destiny.
✅ StrengthHuge sample size; well-controlled natural variation between MZ and DZ
❌ WeaknessMZ twins may share more similar environments too (confounding). 100% concordance never reached.
Caspi et al
2003 · 5-HTT Gene & Depression
Genetics Gene-Environment
⭐
AimTo test whether serotonin transporter gene (5-HTT) interacts with stress to cause depression
TypeLongitudinal prospective study
Participants1,037 individuals followed from birth to age 26 (Dunedin cohort)
ProcedureGenotyped for 5-HTT alleles (short/long); tracked stressful life events and depression outcomes
ResultsThose with two short alleles who experienced stress were most likely to develop depression — gene-environment interaction
Memory Hook"Short gene + bad life = depression risk." Genes load the gun; environment pulls the trigger.
✅ StrengthLongitudinal — shows development over time; large cohort tracked from birth
❌ WeaknessCorrelation only; other genes/factors not controlled; specific to one culture (New Zealand)

Cognitive Approach

Memory models, schema theory, cognitive bias, thinking styles, emotion & cognition

🌟 Priority Studies
Loftus & Palmer
1974 · Car Crash Video · Schema / Reconstructive Memory
Schema Theory Reconstructive Memory
⭐
AimTo test whether the wording of questions affects recall of a car accident
TypeLab experiment (independent measures)
Participants45 students (Experiment 1); 150 students (Experiment 2)
ProcedureWatched a car accident video; asked "how fast were the cars going when they [hit / smashed / collided]?" One week later, asked if they saw broken glass (there was none).
Results"Smashed" group estimated highest speed AND more falsely recalled broken glass — schemas altered memory
Memory Hook"Smash the memory." One word changes both speed estimate and false memories — language reshapes reconstruction.
✅ StrengthHighly controlled lab; clear causal link between wording and memory distortion
❌ WeaknessLow ecological validity (video ≠ real accident); student sample limits generalizability
Brewer & Treyens
1981 · The Office Study · Schema Theory
Schema Theory Reconstructive Memory
⭐
AimTo test whether office schemas affect what people remember being in an office
TypeLab experiment
Participants86 university students
ProcedureWaited in a grad student's office (35 objects; some schema-consistent, some not). Unexpectedly asked to recall what was in the room.
ResultsMore likely to recall schema-consistent items (desk, chair) and falsely recall books. Unusual items (skull, toy) also remembered — due to novelty.
Memory Hook"You remember what belongs in an office." Schemas fill memory gaps with expected objects.
✅ StrengthDemonstrates schema activation naturally; controlled environment
❌ WeaknessArtificial office context; student sample; demand characteristics possible
Glanzer & Cunitz
1966 · Serial Position Effect · Memory Model
Memory Models
AimTo provide evidence for the Multi-Store Model (separate STM and LTM stores)
TypeLab experiment
Participants240 US Army enlisted men
ProcedureShown a list of words; recalled immediately OR after a 10/30-second delay with a distracting task
ResultsPrimacy effect (first words — LTM) stayed. Recency effect (last words — STM) disappeared after delay. Confirms separate stores.
Memory Hook"Delay kills recency." First words stay (LTM); last words vanish when you make them wait (STM disrupted).
✅ StrengthClean empirical support for the Multi-Store Model; highly replicable
❌ WeaknessArtificial word list ≠ real memory; all-male military sample reduces generalizability
Tversky & Kahneman
1974 · Heuristics & Cognitive Bias
Cognitive Bias Intuitive Thinking
⭐
AimTo show that humans use mental shortcuts (heuristics) that lead to predictable errors
TypeSeries of lab experiments / cognitive tasks
ParticipantsVarious samples (students and professionals) across multiple studies
ProcedureParticipants given probability problems and estimation tasks (e.g., anchor-and-adjust: guess if wheel lands on 65, estimate African nations in UN)
ResultsAnchor number systematically skewed estimates; identified availability, representativeness, and anchoring heuristics
Memory Hook"Your first number sticks." Anchoring bias = you can't ignore the first number you hear.
✅ StrengthHighly replicable findings; huge influence on behavioral economics
❌ WeaknessMostly lab-based; real-world decisions are more complex; WEIRD sample
Brown & Kulik
1977 · Flashbulb Memory · Emotion & Cognition
Emotion & Memory
AimTo explore whether emotionally significant events produce vivid, long-lasting "flashbulb" memories
TypeSurvey / self-report study
Participants80 US adults (40 Black, 40 White)
ProcedureParticipants asked to recall their memories of shocking events (e.g., JFK assassination, MLK assassination)
ResultsVivid, detailed memories of shocking events — particularly for events personally relevant to participant group. Coined the term "flashbulb memory."
Memory Hook"Shock = snapshot." Emotional relevance determines flashbulb memory strength.
✅ StrengthIntroduced an influential concept; explored cultural differences in memory
❌ WeaknessSelf-report only — accuracy of memories not verified; retrospective

Sociocultural Approach

Social Identity, stereotypes, cultural dimensions, enculturation, acculturation

🌟 Priority Studies
Tajfel et al
1971 · Minimal Groups · Social Identity Theory
Social Identity Theory Stereotypes
⭐
AimTo test if simply being assigned to a group is enough to trigger in-group favoritism
TypeLab experiment
Participants64 schoolboys from Bristol, UK (aged 14–15)
ProcedureBoys randomly assigned to groups based on a meaningless preference (Klee vs. Kandinsky paintings). Then allocated points to anonymous in-group and out-group members.
ResultsBoys consistently gave more points to their own group — even at the cost of overall profit. Discrimination emerged from minimal group membership alone.
Memory Hook"Even a fake group creates real bias." Mere categorization = in-group favoritism.
✅ StrengthHighly controlled; isolates the variable of group membership from prior relationships
❌ WeaknessOnly male participants; schoolboys only; point allocation may not reflect real-world discrimination
Hilliard & Liben
2010 · Gender Stereotypes in Classroom · Enculturation
Stereotypes Enculturation SIT
⭐
AimTo test whether a teacher emphasizing gender categories increases gender stereotyping in children
TypeField experiment
Participants~60 children in kindergarten classrooms (USA)
ProcedureOne class: teacher used gender labels ("Good morning boys and girls") and gender-segregated activities. Control: no gender emphasis. Assessed after 2 weeks.
ResultsGender-emphasized group showed more gender stereotyping, stronger in-group preferences, and remembered more about their own gender group
Memory Hook"Teachers teach stereotypes too." Labeling genders makes children more biased.
✅ StrengthField experiment — high ecological validity; real children in real school setting
❌ WeaknessSmall sample; cannot control all variables in a classroom; ethical concerns about influencing children
Steele & Aronson
1995 · Stereotype Threat · Effects of Stereotypes
Effects of Stereotypes
AimTo test whether activating a racial stereotype harms academic performance
TypeLab experiment
Participants114 Black and White American university students
ProcedureParticipants completed verbal GRE questions. Half told it was a "test of verbal ability" (stereotype threat condition); others told it was a "problem-solving task."
ResultsBlack students performed significantly worse when told it was an ability test. No difference in the non-diagnostic condition.
Memory Hook"The threat of being seen as dumb makes you score lower." Stereotype awareness disrupts performance.
✅ StrengthControlled experimental conditions; directly tests stereotype threat mechanism
❌ WeaknessUS-specific cultural context; lab performance ≠ real exam; may cause distress in participants
Berry
1967 · Conformity Across Cultures · Cultural Dimensions
Cultural Dimensions
AimTo test whether conformity rates differ between individualist and collectivist cultures
TypeCross-cultural experiment (based on Asch paradigm)
ParticipantsTemne (Sierra Leone — collectivist) and Inuit (Canada — individualist)
ProcedureParticipants completed an Asch-style line judgment task in their own cultural setting
ResultsTemne showed much higher conformity than Inuit — consistent with collectivist vs. individualist cultural values
Memory Hook"Farmers follow, hunters think alone." Agricultural collectivists conform more than nomadic individualists.
✅ StrengthCross-cultural design adds ecological validity; tests cultural dimension theory in action
❌ WeaknessConfounding variables (resource availability, history); may reflect response bias not just conformity
Drury et al
2009 · Crowd Behavior · Social Identity Theory
Social Identity Theory
AimTo explore whether shared social identity explains cooperation in crowd disasters
TypeQualitative interviews (retrospective accounts)
ParticipantsSurvivors of various crowd disasters (e.g., Bradford fire, Hillsborough)
ProcedureIn-depth interviews asking survivors how they felt/behaved in crisis; coded for themes of shared identity
ResultsSurvivors reported helping strangers they felt were "in the same boat" — shared identity increased prosocial behavior under threat
Memory Hook"Same crisis = same team." Disasters create a temporary in-group that motivates helping.
✅ StrengthReal-world ecological validity; captures genuine behavior in extreme conditions
❌ WeaknessRetrospective recall bias; qualitative — hard to generalize; researcher interpretation subjective
Miranda & Matheny
2000 · Acculturation & Stress · Acculturation
Acculturation
AimTo examine predictors of acculturative stress among Latino immigrants in the US
TypeCorrelational survey study
ParticipantsLatino immigrants in the southeastern United States
ProcedureMeasured acculturation level, stress, coping strategies, and English proficiency via questionnaires
ResultsLanguage barriers and weak social networks were key predictors of acculturative stress; coping skills helped reduce it
Memory Hook"Language = lifeline." Without language and community, immigrants suffer more.
✅ StrengthLarge sample of a real-world immigrant population; practically applicable
❌ WeaknessSelf-report — social desirability bias; correlational — no causation

Key Definitions

Define these in your introduction — always link to the question prompt

🧬 Biological
Neuroplasticity
The brain's ability to change its structure and function in response to experience, learning, injury, or environmental stimulation throughout a person's life.
Localization of Function
The idea that specific areas of the brain are responsible for specific behaviors or cognitive processes (e.g., Broca's area → speech production).
Neurotransmitter
A chemical messenger that transmits signals across a synapse from one neuron to another. Examples: acetylcholine (memory), serotonin (mood), dopamine (reward).
Hormone
A chemical substance produced by an endocrine gland and transported in the blood to a target organ to regulate physiological processes and behavior (e.g., cortisol — the stress hormone).
Pheromone
A chemical signal released by one organism that affects the behavior or physiology of others of the same species, often influencing reproductive and social behavior.
Gene-Environment Interaction
When the effect of a gene on behavior depends on the environment, and vice versa. Neither nature nor nurture alone determines the outcome — both interact.
Neural Pruning
The process by which unused or weak synaptic connections are eliminated during brain development, strengthening active neural networks and increasing efficiency.
Agonist
A substance that binds to a receptor and mimics or enhances the effect of a neurotransmitter (e.g., nicotine acts as an acetylcholine agonist).
Antagonist
A substance that blocks or inhibits the action of a neurotransmitter by binding to its receptor without activating it (e.g., scopolamine blocks acetylcholine).
MRI / fMRI
MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging) shows brain structure. fMRI (functional MRI) shows real-time brain activity by measuring blood oxygen levels. Both are non-invasive brain imaging techniques.
🧠 Cognitive
Schema
A mental framework or structure that organizes knowledge and guides the perception and interpretation of new information. Schemas can cause memory distortions when new information is assimilated to fit existing knowledge.
Reconstructive Memory
The idea that memory is not a perfect recording but is rebuilt (reconstructed) each time we recall it, influenced by existing knowledge, expectations, and post-event information.
Flashbulb Memory
A vivid, detailed, and long-lasting memory of a personally significant or emotionally arousing event, proposed by Brown & Kulik (1977). Named for its "photograph-like" quality.
Cognitive Bias
A systematic pattern of deviation from rationality in judgment. Biases emerge when people use heuristics (mental shortcuts) that lead to predictable errors.
Heuristic
A mental shortcut used to make quick decisions or judgments. While often efficient, heuristics can produce systematic errors (e.g., anchoring, availability, representativeness).
Multi-Store Model (MSM)
Atkinson & Shiffrin's model proposing three separate memory stores: Sensory Memory → Short-Term Memory (STM) → Long-Term Memory (LTM), with information transferred through attention and rehearsal.
Rational (Controlled) Thinking
Slow, deliberate, analytical reasoning that requires effort and conscious thought. Corresponds to "System 2" in Kahneman's Dual Process Theory.
Intuitive (Automatic) Thinking
Fast, unconscious, effortless processing that relies on heuristics and prior experience. Corresponds to "System 1" in Kahneman's Dual Process Theory.
🌍 Sociocultural
Social Identity Theory (SIT)
Tajfel & Turner's theory that people derive self-esteem from their membership in social groups (in-groups), leading to in-group favoritism and out-group discrimination to maintain a positive social identity.
Social Cognitive Theory (SCT)
Bandura's theory that behavior is learned through observation, imitation, and modeling of others, mediated by mental processes such as attention, motivation, and self-efficacy.
Stereotype
An oversimplified, generalized belief about the characteristics of a group of people. Can be formed through categorization, media, or cultural transmission, and can affect behavior through stereotype threat.
Stereotype Threat
The risk of confirming a negative stereotype about one's group, which creates anxiety that impairs performance on tasks associated with that stereotype (Steele & Aronson, 1995).
Enculturation
The process by which an individual learns the values, norms, behaviors, and practices of their culture of origin, typically during childhood, through socialization agents like family, school, and media.
Acculturation
The process of cultural and psychological change that results from contact between individuals from different cultures. Strategies include integration, assimilation, separation, and marginalization (Berry's model).
Cultural Dimension
Broad value orientations that distinguish cultures from one another. Hofstede's dimensions include individualism-collectivism, power distance, uncertainty avoidance, and masculinity-femininity.
In-group / Out-group
In-group: the social group you belong to and identify with. Out-group: groups you do not belong to. SIT predicts in-group favoritism (we favor our own group) and out-group derogation.
